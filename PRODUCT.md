# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Recruiters, hiring managers, and collaborators (PM, UX design, UX research roles) evaluating Thomas Richardson's work. They're scanning quickly — context is a resume/LinkedIn click-through — and need to judge craft, thinking process, and range (technical UX work vs. advertising/strategy work) within seconds, then dig into a case study if interested.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio site presenting Thomas Richardson as a Creative PM & UX Designer who bridges advertising strategy and product thinking. It showcases project case studies (Math Center Kiosk Redesign, Vivacity Tech work, Pokédex, Ralph Lauren media plan, Gray Owl Coffee research) split across a "Technical" and "Advertising" lane — a deliberate split-brain framing of his dual skill set. Success is a visitor understanding his range and reaching out (resume download, email, LinkedIn) or reading a full case study.

## Brand Personality

Precise, warm, confident. Analytical and detail-oriented (UX research rigor, accessibility-mindedness) without feeling cold or corporate; confident about the work without overselling. Voice is direct and specific (project descriptions state real numbers/outcomes: "20+ modals," "5 components with 100+ variants," "1st Place"), with small personal touches (Tokyo clock, greeting language easter egg) that add warmth without undercutting credibility.

## Anti-references

Generic template-y portfolio sites: interchangeable Squarespace/Framer-template layouts, identical icon+heading+text card grids, tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows above every section, numbered 01/02/03 section scaffolding, gradient text, glassmorphism-as-decoration, hero-metric clichés. The site should read as authored by this specific person, not generated from a portfolio template.

## Design Principles

- Show, don't tell — lead with real project outcomes and specifics, not adjectives.
- Two lanes, one voice — the tech/advertising split ("split brain") is the organizing device; keep both lanes visually distinct (navy vs. rust) but tonally consistent.
- Practice what you preach — as a UX/accessibility-minded designer, the site itself must model accessible, well-crafted execution.
- Warmth in the details, not the surface — personality comes through small specific touches (clock, language greeting), not decorative flourish.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Standard WCAG AA. No specific known user accommodations beyond that; reduced-motion support and keyboard/focus handling should meet general best practice.
